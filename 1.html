Q1 PDF 1 — Q.136 Quantization of Charge
5 Marks
If the protons and electrons are the only basic charges in the universe, all the observable charges have to be integral multiples of e. Thus, if an object contains x electrons and y protons, the net charge on the object will be:
1. -(x+y)e
2. (x+y)e
3. (x-y)e
4. (y-x)e

📖 Complete Solution

Electron has charge = -e (negative)
Proton has charge = +e (positive)
x electrons → total charge = x × (-e) = -xe
y protons → total charge = y × (+e) = +ye
Net Charge = -xe + ye = (y - x)e
✅ Answer: Option 4 → (y-x)e

⚠️ Common mistake: Students write (x-y)e → but electrons are negative, protons are positive. Always: Protons MINUS Electrons.

✅ Answer: (B) -2e → Net = (8-10)e = -2e
Q2 PDF 1 — Q.143 Coulomb's Law
5 Marks
Two point charges placed a distance d apart in vacuum exert a force of magnitude F on each other. One of the two charges is doubled. To keep the magnitude of force same, the separation between the charges should be changed to:
1. 2d
2. d/2
3. √2 d
4. d/√2

📖 Complete Solution

F = kq₁q₂/d²
Initially: F = kq₁q₂/d²
After change: One charge doubled → q₁ becomes 2q₁, new distance = d'
New force: F' = k(2q₁)q₂/d'²
For F' = F → k(2q₁)q₂/d'² = kq₁q₂/d²
→ 2/d'² = 1/d² → d'² = 2d² → d' = √2 × d
New separation = √2 d
✅ Answer: Option 3 → √2 d

(A) d
(B) 2d
(C) d/2
(D) 4d

Q4 PDF 2 — Q.645 Coulomb's Law - Distance
5 Marks
A point charge q is placed at a distance r from another point charge Q. If the distance between the charges is doubled then:
1. Force becomes half, PE becomes one-fourth
2. Force becomes one-fourth, PE becomes half
3. Both force and PE become one-fourth
4. Both force and PE become half

📖 Complete Solution

Force: F = kQq/r² → when r→2r: F' = kQq/(2r)² = kQq/4r² = F/4
Potential Energy: U = kQq/r → when r→2r: U' = kQq/2r = U/2
Force ∝ 1/r² → doubles distance = 1/4 force
PE ∝ 1/r → doubles distance = 1/2 PE
✅ Answer: Option 2 → Force = F/4, PE = U/2

⚠️ Most common CUET trap: Force and PE have DIFFERENT distance dependence!

Q5 PDF 3 — Q.2 3-Charge Equilibrium
5 Marks
Two point charges (-q) and (+4q) are placed at separation 'r'. Where should a third charge be placed so that entire system of charges becomes in equilibrium?
1. At separation 'r' from (-q) on the extreme side of -q
2. At separation 'r' from (4q) on the extreme side of 4q
3. At r/2 from (-q) in between the two charges
4. At r/4 from (4q) in between the two charges

📖 Complete Solution

For 3-charge equilibrium, third charge must be placed such that force on ALL charges = 0
Since charges are opposite sign (-q and +4q), third charge must be OUTSIDE (on the side of smaller charge)
Let third charge Q be at distance x from -q, outside on -q side
Force on -q from +4q: F₁ = k(4q)(q)/r² (attractive, towards +4q)
For equilibrium of -q: force from Q must balance this → Q must be positive (repulsion from -q side)
Solving: x = r (from -q) → third charge = -4q/9 at distance r from -q
Position: At distance r from (-q) on extreme side of -q
✅ Answer: Option 1 → At separation r from (-q) on extreme side

Q6 PDF 3 — Q.1 E Field - Infinite Wire
5 Marks
An infinitely long wire is charged uniformly with charge density λ and placed in air. The electric field at distance r from wire will be:
1. (1/4πε₀)(λ/r)
2. (1/4πε₀)(λ/r²)
3. λ/2ε₀
4. λ/2πε₀r

📖 Complete Solution — Using Gauss's Law

Draw a cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius r and length L around the wire
Charge enclosed: Q_enc = λL
Flux through cylinder: φ = E × (2πrL) [only curved surface contributes]
By Gauss's Law: φ = Q_enc/ε₀
E × 2πrL = λL/ε₀
E = λ/2πε₀r
Note: E ∝ 1/r (NOT 1/r² like point charge)
✅ Answer: Option 4 → E = λ/2πε₀r

Q7 PDF 3 — Q.3 E vs r Graph - Conducting Shell
5 Marks
The variation of electric field with respect to distance from centre of a charged conducting spherical shell of radius R is given by: (Which graph is correct?)
1. Linear increase throughout
2. E=0 inside (r<R), peaks at R, then falls as 1/r² outside
3. Increases then decreases (peak inside)
4. Increases linearly then becomes zero

📖 Complete Solution

For r < R (inside shell): By Gauss's Law, no charge enclosed → E = 0
For r = R (on surface): E = kQ/R² → Maximum value
For r > R (outside): E = kQ/r² → Decreases as 1/r²
E = 0     (r < R)
E = kQ/R²   (r = R) ← maximum
E = kQ/r²   (r > R) ← falls
✅ Answer: Option 2 → Zero inside, peak at R, falls as 1/r²

⚠️ This is the MOST ASKED graph in CUET! Remember: conducting shell has all charge on surface.

3. 6×10⁻⁵ J

📖 Complete Solution

Q = 3×10⁻⁷ C (source charge), q = 2×10⁻⁹ C (test charge)

Numerator = 9×10⁹ × 6×10⁻¹⁶ = 54×10⁻⁷ = 5.4×10⁻⁶

Q15 PDF 1 — Q.142 Dipole Potential — Properties
5 Marks
The electric potential due to an electric dipole:
(A) depends on r (magnitude of position vector)
(B) depends on angle between position vector and dipole moment
(C) falls off at long distances as 1/r²
(D) does not depend upon the distance separating the charges

2. (A), (B) and (C) only

📖 Complete Solution

V = kp cosθ / r²

✅ Answer: Option 2 → A, B and C only

Q17 PDF 3 — Q.6 Torque on Dipole
5 Marks
The expression for torque 'τ' experienced by an electric dipole of dipole moment 'P' in an external uniform electric field 'E' is given by:

A dipole in electric field experiences a torque that tries to align it with the field

Q18 PDF 1 — Q.138 Series Capacitors
5 Marks
In a series combination of capacitors connected across a battery:
1. Each capacitor has equal charge for certain values of capacitances only
2. Each capacitor has different charge for a certain value of its capacitance
3. Each capacitor has equal charge for any value of its capacitance

📖 Complete Solution

In series combination, capacitors are connected end-to-end
There is only ONE path for charge flow
The charge that leaves one plate must equal charge on other plate
Q₁ = Q₂ = Q₃ = Q (SAME charge for ALL capacitors in series)
This is TRUE regardless of the values of capacitances!
Voltage may differ: V = Q/C (different V for different C)
✅ Answer: Option 3 → Equal charge for ANY capacitance value

📌 Remember: Series → Same Q | Parallel → Same V

1. ε₀A/2d
2. ε₀A/(d-b)
3. 2ε₀A/(d+b/2)
4. ε₀A/(d+b/2)

📖 Complete Solution

Copper is a CONDUCTOR (not dielectric)
Inside conductor: E = 0 → copper plate contributes NO electric field region
Effective gap = d - b (total separation minus conductor thickness)
C = ε₀A/(d-b)
Note: For dielectric (non-conductor) of thickness t: C = ε₀A/(d - t + t/K)
For conductor (K→∞): C = ε₀A/(d - b + 0) = ε₀A/(d-b)
✅ Answer: Option 2 → C = ε₀A/(d-b)

⚠️ Conductor inserted → C increases (effective d decreases)

Q21 PDF 2 — Q.681 Capacitors in Series + Dielectric
5 Marks
Two parallel plate capacitors, each of capacitance 40 μF are connected in series. The space between the plates of one capacitor is filled with a material of dielectric constant K=4. The equivalent capacitance of the system would be:
1. 30 μF
2. 31 μF
3. 32 μF
4. 33 μF

📖 Complete Solution

C₁ = 40 μF (without dielectric)
C₂ = K × 40 = 4 × 40 = 160 μF (with dielectric K=4)
Series combination: 1/C_eq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂
1/C_eq = 1/40 + 1/160 = 4/160 + 1/160 = 5/160
C_eq = 160/5 = 32 μF
✅ Answer: Option 3 → 32 μF
