A1 — Reflection Laws & Mirror Formula

PDF1 Q168 PDF3 Q40
1/v + 1/u = 1/f  |  f = R/2  |  m = -v/u = h_i/h_o

📐 New Cartesian Sign Convention

→All distances measured from pole of mirror/lens
+Distances in direction of incident light = positive
−Distances opposite to incident light = negative
🔴For mirrors: object distance u is always negative
🔵Concave mirror: f = negative | Convex mirror: f = positive
🔴 Concave Mirror

• Converging mirror
• f = negative
• Can form real & virtual images
• Used: headlights, telescopes, dentist

🔵 Convex Mirror

• Diverging mirror
• f = positive
• Always forms virtual, erect, diminished image
• Used: rear view mirror, security

✅All mirrors follow laws of reflection: angle of incidence = angle of reflection
📌Rays parallel to principal axis → reflect through focus of curved mirror
⚡Rays at pole → reflect making equal angle with axis on other side

A2 — Refraction, Snell's Law & Critical Angle

PDF1 Q172,174 PDF2 Q654
n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r  (Snell's Law)
sin i_c = n₂/n₁  (Critical angle: n₁ > n₂)
📌When light goes from denser→rarer medium, it bends away from normal
📌Critical angle i_c: angle of incidence for which angle of refraction = 90°
🔴TIR occurs when: (1) denser→rarer medium AND (2) i > i_c
ConditionResult
Rarer → Denser (any angle)Refraction occurs (no TIR)
Denser → Rarer, i < i_cPartial refraction + reflection
Denser → Rarer, i = i_cRefracted ray along surface (r=90°)
Denser → Rarer, i > i_cTotal Internal Reflection (TIR) ✅
📋 PDF 1 — Q.172 PDF 1 Critical Angle Definition
The critical angle of incidence i_c for a ray incident from a denser to rarer medium, is that angle for which:
1. The angle of reflection is 90°
2. The angle of refraction is 90° ✅
3. The angle of refraction is 0°
4. The angle of reflection is 0°
Critical angle i_c: the special angle of incidence (in denser medium)
At critical angle → refracted ray travels along the surface = angle of refraction = 90°
sin(i_c) = n₂/n₁ (derived from Snell's law when r=90°, sin90°=1)
✅ Answer: Option 2 → Angle of refraction = 90°
📋 PDF 1 — Q.174 PDF 1 TIR — Correct Statements
(A) TIR occurs when a ray travels from rarer to denser medium
(B) In TIR, incident ray stays in same medium after reflection
(C) In TIR, angle of incidence = angle of reflection inside denser medium
(D) In TIR inside denser medium, there is no angle of refraction
1. (B) and (C) only
2. (A) and (B) only
3. (B), (C) and (D) only ✅
4. (C) and (D) only
(A) FALSE — TIR occurs denser→rarer (NOT rarer→denser)
(B) TRUE — In TIR, light stays in same (denser) medium
(C) TRUE — TIR follows reflection laws: i = r
(D) TRUE — In TIR, no refracted ray exists, so no refraction angle
✅ Answer: Option 3 → B, C and D only
📋 PDF 2 — Q.654 PDF 2 TIR — Water/Glass Interface
Light is incident on an interface between water (μ=4/3) and glass (μ=3/2). For total internal reflection, light should be travelling from:
1. Water to glass and ∠i > i_c
2. Water to glass and ∠i < i_c
3. Glass to water and ∠i < i_c
4. Glass to water and ∠i > i_c ✅
μ_water = 4/3 = 1.33 | μ_glass = 3/2 = 1.5
Glass is denser (higher μ) than water
TIR: light must travel denser→rarer = Glass→Water
AND angle must be greater than critical angle: i > i_c
✅ Answer: Option 4 → Glass to Water, i > i_c

A3 — Lenses: Focal Length, Power & Lens Maker's Equation

PDF1 Q169,170,175 PDF3 Q39
1/f = 1/v - 1/u  (Lens formula)
P = 1/f (metres)  |  1/f = (n-1)[1/R₁ - 1/R₂]  (Lens maker's)
Convex Lens

• Converging | f = positive
• Power P = positive
• Can form real & virtual images
• Used: camera, microscope, eye

Concave Lens

• Diverging | f = negative
• Power P = negative
• Always virtual, erect, diminished
• Used: specs for myopia

🎯 CUET Important Facts about Power of Lens:
(A) Power = ability to converge/diverge rays ✅
(B) SI unit = Dioptre (D), when f is in METRES ✅
(C) Larger focal length → SMALLER power ✅
(D) Combined lenses: P_total = P₁ + P₂ (algebraic sum) ✅

(C) For lens of larger focal length, power is smaller

1. (A) and (C) only ✅
2. (B), (C) and (D) only
3. (A) and (B) only
4. (A) only
(A) TRUE — Power = 1/f = ability to converge/diverge

A4 — Prism & Minimum Deviation

PDF2 Q679
μ = sin[(A+D_m)/2] / sin(A/2)  |  δ = (μ-1)A (thin prism)
📌At minimum deviation: r₁ = r₂ (ray passes symmetrically through prism)
📌For equilateral prism: A = 60°
📋 PDF 2 — Q.679 PDF 2 Equilateral Prism
An equilateral prism is made of material of refractive index √3. Angle of minimum deviation through the prism is:
(1) 60° ✅
(2) 30°
(3) 45°
(4) 0°
μ = √3, A = 60° (equilateral)
μ = sin[(A+D_m)/2] / sin(A/2) = sin[(60+D_m)/2] / sin30°
√3 = sin[(60+D_m)/2] / 0.5
sin[(60+D_m)/2] = √3/2 = sin60°
(60+D_m)/2 = 60° → 60+D_m = 120° → D_m = 60°
✅ Answer: D_m = 60°

B1 — Young's Double Slit Experiment (YDSE)

PDF1 Q171 PDF2 Q656 PDF3 Q41-45
β = λD/d  (fringe width)
Path diff for bright: nλ  |  Path diff for dark: (2n-1)λ/2
Variables

β = fringe width
λ = wavelength of light
D = distance slit to screen
d = slit separation

Key Effects

β ∝ λ (more λ → wider fringes)
β ∝ D (more D → wider fringes)
β ∝ 1/d (more d → narrower fringes)
In water: λ decreases → β decreases

CUET Trick: When apparatus immersed in water (n=4/3): λ_water = λ_air/n = 3λ/4. So β_water = β_air × (3/4) = 0.75 β_air
⭐Constructive interference (bright): Phase diff Δφ = 2nπ where n = 0,1,2,3...
⭐Destructive interference (dark): Phase diff Δφ = (2n-1)π where n = 1,2,3...
📋 PDF 1 — Q.171 PDF 1 Fringe Width Numerical
In YDSE, two slits are 1.5mm apart while the screen is 1.2m away. Light of wavelength 600nm is incident. Fringe width will be:
1. 0.48 mm ✅
2. 4.5 mm
3. 4.8 mm
4. 4.2 mm
λ = 600nm = 600×10⁻⁹m, D = 1.2m, d = 1.5mm = 1.5×10⁻³m
β = λD/d = (600×10⁻⁹ × 1.2) / (1.5×10⁻³)
β = 720×10⁻⁹ / 1.5×10⁻³ = 480×10⁻⁶ m = 0.48 mm
✅ Answer: 0.48 mm
📋 PDF 2 — Q.656 PDF 2 Path Difference for I₀/2
In YDSE using monochromatic light λ, intensity at a point with path difference λ is I₀. Path difference where intensity is I₀/2 will be:
(1) λ/4 ✅
(2) λ/2
(3) λ
(4) 2λ
I = I₀ cos²(Δφ/2). When path diff = λ → Δφ = 2π → I = I₀cos²(π) = I₀ (maxima)
For I = I₀/2: cos²(Δφ/2) = 1/2 → cos(Δφ/2) = 1/√2 → Δφ/2 = π/4 → Δφ = π/2
Path difference = λ × Δφ/2π = λ × (π/2)/2π = λ/4
✅ Answer: λ/4
📋

PDF 3 — Q.41 to Q.45 — YDSE Case Based (5 Questions) PDF 3

Q.41 — Phase difference for constructive interference:
(2) Δφ = 2nπ ; n = 0, 2, 3, 4, 5 ✅
Bright fringe: Δφ = 2nπ (n = 0,1,2,...) → path diff = nλ
Q.42 — If 2 sources of intensity I₀ each have randomly varying phase, resultant intensity:
(3) 2I₀ ✅
Random phase → I = I₁ + I₂ = I₀ + I₀ = 2I₀ (no interference term)
Q.43 — If one slit is closed in YDSE:
(3) Intensity of central max decreases, width of central max increases ✅
One slit closed → diffraction from single slit (not interference)
Intensity decreases (less light) but diffraction spreads wider
Q.44 — Slit separation halved, screen distance doubled. Fringe width:
(4) Quadrupled ✅
β = λD/d. New β' = λ(2D)/(d/2) = 4λD/d = 4β → Quadrupled!
Q.45 — Apparatus in water (n=4/3), fringe width was 0.4mm. New fringe width:
(3) 0.3 mm ✅
β_water = β_air / n = 0.4 / (4/3) = 0.4 × 3/4 = 0.3 mm

B2 — Polarisation & Malus's Law

PDF1 Q173
I = I₀ cos²θ  (Malus's Law)
θ = angle between polariser axis and analyser axis
📌Unpolarised light → passes through polariser → becomes plane polarised
📌Two crossed polaroids (θ=90°): I = I₀cos²90° = 0 (no light passes)
✅Polaroid sheet rotated between two crossed polarisers → max I when θ=45° from first polariser
📋 PDF 1 — Q.173 PDF 1 Polaroid — Max Intensity
A polaroid sheet is rotated between two crossed polarizers. The intensity of transmitted light would be maximum when the angle between the axes of the first polarizer and the polaroid sheet is:
1. π/2
2. π/4 ✅
3. π
4. π/3
Let polaroid sheet make angle θ with P1. Second polaroid P2 is at 90° to P1.
After P1: I₁ = I₀/2 (unpolarised → polarised)
After polaroid sheet: I₂ = I₁ cos²θ
After P2 (at 90°-θ from sheet): I₃ = I₂ cos²(90°-θ) = I₁ cos²θ sin²θ
I₃ = (I₁/4) sin²(2θ) → maximum when sin²2θ = 1 → 2θ = 90° → θ = 45° = π/4
✅ Answer: π/4 (45°)

A) I₀/8 ✅
B) I₀/4
C) I₀/2
D) 3I₀/8
After polariser: I₁=I₀/2. After analyser: I = I₁cos²60° = (I₀/2)(1/4) = I₀/8
